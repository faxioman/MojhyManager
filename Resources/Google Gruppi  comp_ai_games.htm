I don't know about this information for commercial soccer games.
However, if you're interested in this you may also be interested in some
variations.  There are robotic and software simulation versions of a
soccer tournament held internationally to test state of the art research
in AI.  This is called Robocup, and you can get information from
http://www.google.com/url?sa=D&q=http://www.robocup.org

Or you can look at a highly simplified "mini-soccer simulation" that I
developed (inspired by a similar simulation created by Tucker Balch).
You can find out about this at
http://www.google.com/url?sa=D&q=http://www.cs.colby.edu/~rjones/tclsoccer/

From that web site you can get the code for the soccer simulator
(written in tcl/tk) as well as some sample AI teams.  I know of some
learning teams (using neural nets, among other things) for this
simulator, but I don't have the code at hand.  It's possible I could get
the code for those teams and put it on the web if there's enough
interest.

Each player has a state at any time. It could be dribbling, shooting,
defending, etc. (you need to work
out the actual states yourself). Rules are used to transition between the
states. For example
IF I'm close to the goal THEN shoot.
Of course it is a little more complicated than that (there's no need to
shoot 100% of the time),
but it should give you an idea to get started.
Especially teamwork needs to be considered at a different level.
"Design Patterns" is a book by Erich Gamma et al., and the State pattern is
very useful here,
especially because it reduces the number of rules:
You don't have to say "if I have the ball and I'm close to the goal, shoot"
because in the dribbling
state you know you have the ball (otherwise you wouldn't be in the dribbling
state).

I'm sorry I can't go into more details, but these really are fundamentals
and can be found on the web.
To translate the fundamentals into soccer stuff would be your job.

I would consider a few things in the Ai design..

Yes, states would be good.
The player needs to know a bit about his position, other wise you will have
them moving all over the place..Hey RUGBY!!
So a attacker on the wing might stay towards the side and up front in his
movement pattern.

Players also need to be able to see what is going on around them. So, he must
make some decisions

I have a clear shot at the goal, I will shoot.
My friend is near the goal, I will assist.
I have a man open up field, I will do a long pass.
I have too many defenders near me, maybe I will pass back.
There are two many defenders near my friend with the ball, I will support him
or "get open"

In soccer, you usually have to be on some type of angle to get a good pas. So
think on this.

I recommend you make skill levels for different attributes.

So a player could be good at dribbling..aka Ronaldo..His Ai will make him hold
on to the ball more.
Perhaps the player is quick, his AI will make him do fast runs. etc..

Also AI for the team..Will they be agreesive, or passive. Do they attack often?
Do they play 'long ball.'

One thing I wouldn't do is allow a player to see ALL his team mates. That's not
realistic. In soccer, you usually don't see everyone. So limit the players
vision. If the tempo of the game is slow, perhaps you can increase his 'vision'
Or if he has a high skill level increase this attribute.

It would be good for the Ai to "learn" a teams weakness. If they can remember,
then they can play even smarter.

For example, your attackers learn that your right fullback is too slow, they
might make runs on that side more often.

As someone suggested, there is a great paper on soccer Ai with Robots.. Any
other ideas, please send them to me.

I'm doing something on this line. My game at present is done on a
heuristic plan, like you mention. Each player has a set of attributes,
such as "opportunism"==the ability to take the ball from another player,
"ability"==the ability to keep the ball against another player,
"precision"==the ability to send the ball where he wants, etc. At each
point, the player "decides" ipon keeping the ball, passing it, shooting
to the goal, etc, based on arithmetic formulas. The simulation is
physically correct, using Euler integration, but I use parameters to
speed it up a bit, to keep it more interesting. The result is like it
was played on a smaller field.

The human interface uses OpenGL. You see a field and you can move the
camera up, down, from side to side, and zoom in and out. I still have to
do animated player figures, there is just a number where the player is,
in this version.

For the future, I'm studying ways to use neural networks + genetic
algorithms. A player will develop his skill by optimizing his neural
network parameters. Successful players will transfer parts of their
networks to lesser players. After watching a lot of games, I came to the
conclusion that heuristic play is not creative enough.

up a website ;D

Most of the work in AI has its roots in the notion of state-space
search.  I can't claim to know what the previous poster meant by
"states", but for any AI systems of any complexity they will be much
more complex than the very general cases you have listed here.

I find it most useful to think of any particular state as a set of
features with particular values.  For a problem of any complexity, there
are going to be a *lot* of possible features.  As an example, here are
the features I used for an AI soccer team for a *very* simple soccer
simultion:

clearToGoal:
   True if there are no opponents that can interfere with my path to the
   opponents' goal

closeOpponent:
   True if any opponent is within a small distance of me

breakaway:
   True if a teammate thinks it has a breakaway to the opponents' goal

dancing:
   True if the players should be dancing (because of a breakaway)

needHelp:
   Counts how many turns the lead has been very close to both the ball
and
   the opponent (indicating that the opponent may be pushing the ball,
or
   the ball is stuck)

staleKick:
   Counts how many turns have passed since anybody on our team kicked
the
   ball (indicating that we should become more offensive to regain
control)

NearObject:
   The direction to any particular object next to me

CloseToOpponent:
   True if there is an opponent close to me in a particular direction

DirectionTo:
   The compass direction from any square to any other square

DistanceTo:
   The minimum number of squares to travel between any two spots

FieldSide:
   Am I on the "left" (south) or "right" (north) side of the field?

BallX:
   The X location of the ball (only computable if I see the ball)

BallY:
   The Y location of the ball (only computable if I see the ball)

KickSouth:
   Which north-south direction is it desirable to kick the ball in,
based
   on the relative north-south positions to me of my teammates, the
   opponents and the north and south edges of the field?

OpenPasses:
   In which directions is there a teammate a few squares away, with no
   opponents close to the teammate, and no opponents possibly blocking a
   kick in that direction?

OpponentAt:
   True if there is an opponent at a particular direction and distance
   from me.

OpponentBlocking:
   True if an opponent is in a particular direction and less than some
   number squares away.

BackPassOkay:
   True if it would be okay to pass the ball NE, E, or SE (currently
never
   true).

ForwardX:
   The proper X position for the forward.

ForwardY:
   The proper Y position for the forward.

ForwardInPosition:
   True if the forward is close to the proper position.

FirstBackX:
   The proper X position for the first back.

FirstBackY:
   The proper Y position for the first back.

SecondBackX:
   The proper X position for the second back.

SecondBackY:
   The proper Y position for the second back.

> This is a point I'd like to discuss deeper. What should the player
> know. In a real soccer game of course, the player knows a lot of
> things, but in an AI soccer game we are bounded by memory requirements
> and processor speeds. So we really should nail the important ones.

For any modern pattern-matching engine (which is what I'd recommend you
use), the memory and processor requirements will not be the issue
(IMHO).  The hard part is deciding all of the features you need for each
state (the above list may give you a start), and then encoding the
knowledge to get the players to achieve there goals in each possible
situation.  Once you've defined all that, it can be implemented quite
efficiently.  I know there are those who would argue that speed and
memory are *always* an issue in modern computer games, because of the
need for sophisticated graphics.  In response to that, I'll concede that
processing cycles can sometimes be an issue, but a competent AI really
shouldn't be in danger of taking up too much memory (compared to, say,
the graphics memory demands).

Randy Jones

Also, the teams play in opposite directions. For team A, attack is
moving towards positive x, team B attacks towards negative x. But
positions should not be strictly relative, because attackers and
defenders need different attitudes. An attacker may risk losing the ball
in order to try to reach the goal; a defender may never risk losing the
ball, so it is best to simply kick the ball forward than risk letting
the enemy pass through.
